late 2024 — the decision

2024
Raleigh, North Carolina
Everything in Raleigh felt like a reminder of something I wanted to forget.

I'd been managing a Backyard Burger for two years. I was going through a breakup — a bad one, the kind that makes everywhere you've ever been feel like evidence — and coming off a rough semester. Raleigh had started to feel like a room I was trapped in. I needed out.

My uncle is a UPS pilot who lives in Anchorage. He has a Cessna 172. Sometime around October he told me to come up in the summer of 2025 and work on my pilot's license. I said yes before he finished the sentence. I'd been to Alaska three times before and I already knew what it does to you — the scale of it, the way the mountains just sit there completely indifferent to your problems. That's what I needed.

I booked the flights around February 21st. Put in my two weeks at Backyard Burger. Told my friends, who were genuinely happy for me. The day I booked it I felt like I could breathe again for the first time in months. My parents were excited too — they'd seen Alaska get into me before. They knew what it meant.

2025
Anchorage, Alaska — arrival
Landed. Didn't know anyone in the city except my uncle. That felt exactly right.

The view flying into Anchorage is the mountains. They just sit there — enormous, close, not caring about you at all. I'd seen them before but it still hit different coming in on that flight knowing I wasn't leaving for a while.

My uncle's house is huge. Three floors, five bedrooms, three-car garage. My cousins Naomi, Sam, and Anna all live in Anchorage too. By March 7th I was already out at the international airport plane spotting. On March 9th I did a snowy loop hike at Eagle River and stood on a frozen lake surrounded by mountains at the end of it. The trail was icy and I had no idea what I was doing and it was perfect.

March in Anchorage is cold but not brutal. The city is smaller than you expect but the wilderness right outside it is bigger than you can really prepare for. Within a week I felt more like myself than I had in months.

Lake Hood, Anchorage
I walked into Regal Air with my resume. The owner hired me in ten minutes.

My uncle found a Regal Air post on Facebook — they were hiring seasonal dock hands at Lake Hood. I didn't know what Lake Hood was yet. It's a floatplane base with over a thousand planes on it, either on the water or on the land around it. If you love aviation, it's the kind of place that makes you stop walking and just stand there for a second.

I walked in on March 11th, talked to the owner for about ten minutes, and got the job. I wouldn't start until the end of May, but I had it. A few days later on March 14th I was already out at my uncle's hangar helping pull the elevator off his Cessna 172 — it had gotten damaged months back and needed to be paint-stripped and repainted before it went back on. Not what I expected to be doing my second week in Alaska but I wasn't complaining.

Anchorage, Alaska
The aurora forecast was high. Me and Naomi drove out and stayed for thirty minutes.

Something worth knowing about northern lights: your camera sees them way better than your naked eye does. The photos look like green curtains draped across the sky. In person they're more subtle — a glow, a shimmer, shapes you're not entirely sure are moving or if you're imagining it. Still incredible. Just different than what you expect from the pictures.

Me and my cousin Naomi drove out to watch them on March 26th. We were out there for about thirty minutes. I have pictures that look nothing like what I actually experienced, which is kind of the point. Some things don't compress into a photo.

may 2025 — flying & working
19
May
2025
Matanuska-Susitna Valley, Alaska
First flight. Two hours in the Mat-Su Valley and I forgot about everything else.

My uncle had been working on the Cessna 172 since we paint-stripped the elevator back in March. Annual inspection was May 13th — I got to help with some of that too. On May 19th we finally flew. Two hours in the Matanuska-Susitna Valley practice area, me at the controls for most of it.

The Mat-Su Valley from the air is wide and flat with mountains on every side. At some point you stop trying to take it in and just fly. I ended up doing three flights total. The second one on May 27th was rainy — flying in the rain over Alaska is its own specific thing, all grey and green below you. I never got close to soloing. I'm not that worried about the license anymore. The summer gave me other things.

On May 3rd I went to the Alaska Aviation Gathering in Palmer with my uncle. Hundreds of planes, people who've been flying their whole lives, planes parked on grass in every direction. That night me and my cousin Sam had a pallet bonfire at Westchester Lagoon on the beach.

june 2025 — parents visit
24
Jun
2025
Anchorage, Girdwood & Whittier, Alaska
My parents flew up. We spent four days doing everything I'd been saving for them.

My parents arrived around June 24th. We hit the Alaska Wildlife Conservation Center first — moose, bears, wolves, caribou. Not exactly things you see anywhere else. We drove to Girdwood and saw Virgin Creek Falls, then Bird Creek Falls. The Alyeska Resort has a gondola that goes up about 2,000 feet — we rode it to the top and stood there looking at the valley. Next day we did two hours of ATV riding through the woods to Bird Creek Falls.

June 26th I took my dad up Rendezvous Peak, just the two of us. That hike meant a lot. Big family bonfire that evening. June 27th we drove to Whittier and got on the 26 Glacier Cruise — seven hours, twenty-six glaciers, waterfalls running down mountainsides straight into the ocean, seals on ice. My dad kept going quiet in a way that told me he finally got it. Why I needed to come here. Why this place is different.

july 2025 — summer peak
04
Jul
2025
Anchorage, Alaska
Mount Magnificent on July 4th. Seven hours. My favorite hike in Alaska, no question.

Three peaks. About 4,000 feet at the top. Seven hours round trip. There's a weather station near the summit that's been sitting up there doing its thing in complete silence while you wheeze up the last stretch. When you get above the treeline and the valley opens up below you — a river running through it, mountains on the other side — it stops feeling like exercise and starts feeling like something else.

I've done a lot of hikes in Alaska by this point. Flat Top at sunset at 11pm when it's still light out, the Powerline Trail, the Eagle River loop in the snow, Byron Glacier where it felt like being inside an ice room. Mount Magnificent is still the one I think about. If you ever find yourself in Anchorage and can only do one hike, start early and go there.

Anchorage, Homer, Denali & Eagle River
Nate flew up for a week. We tried to do everything. We basically did.

My friend Nate got to Anchorage on July 14th. We had five days. Girdwood and the Alyeska gondola first, 2,000 feet up. Pallet bonfire at Lynnary Park that night, met some people, stayed too late.

July 15th we drove all the way to Homer. The Spit is this narrow strip of land that stretches out into the bay with shops and docks packed onto it — surprisingly cool. Got pizza out there. Cooper Landing bonfire on the way back, mountains on every side, fire going, lake calm. July 16th we hiked Mount Magnificent together and then camped at Eagle River right on the river, the sound of it all night. July 17th: Denali, ziplining inside the park. July 18th Nate left. Having a piece of home show up in the middle of all of this was a good feeling.

Katmai National Park, Alaska
My boss handed me a lunch and a jacket and said go. I saw twelve bears.

Showed up to Regal Air at 7am like any other day. There was an empty seat on the Katmai flight. My boss — not the warmest guy, worked me pretty hard all summer — just told me to get on the plane. Two and a half hours each way, four flights total. I had five hours at Brooks Camp.

About twelve bears. I walked the trails alone, playing music out loud on my phone so they could hear me coming, kicking rocks down the path ahead of me. At the falls I watched them fish for fifteen minutes — standing maybe thirty feet away, completely in their own world. I was a dock hand from Raleigh, NC, in a national park in Alaska on a Tuesday because my boss had an empty seat and felt like being generous. The job wasn't always easy — yard work, loading luggage in the rain, taking heat from coworkers who didn't love having me around. Days like this made all of that irrelevant.

Anchorage, Alaska
She DM'd me first on July 5th. By August 5th I was asking her to be my girlfriend on her mattress at 11pm.

I'd noticed Maggie at Sorrento's — we both worked there, I was a host, and I always thought she was beautiful but never really said much. She reached out first on July 5th. I know the exact date because I have a screenshot of her Instagram in my photos from that day.

First hangout was around July 25th — thrifting, got fries somewhere. July 27th we had a bonfire at Lynnary Park at 10pm and stayed until 3am. In Alaska in July, 10pm is still light out. That detail still gets me. August 3rd we took the ambulance out to a pull-off near Girdwood and had our first sleepover. We hiked Bird Creek Falls, built Legos, watched sunsets. On August 5th we went to Lynnary Park and then back to her place where the ambulance was parked. Late, maybe 11pm. We were lying on the mattress and I just asked her. Simple as that. She said yes. I gave her flowers the next day.

september 2025 — the build & departure
28
Sep
2025
Anchorage, Alaska — departure
We finished the ceiling at 8pm. Left at 9. The floor was still covered in bags.

The ambulance was completely empty when we started — bare metal walls, black surfaces, nothing. We built the whole thing in about three weeks in September. Tongue-and-groove floors, walls, a skylight, a raised bed platform, a two-sided fridge, a big power station, five-gallon water drum, curtains. Between the 25th and 26th we finished the bed frame. The ceiling went in on the 28th. We each got sick at least three times. Eight urgent care visits between us. I got tonsillitis. We kept building.

We left at 9pm. The floor was still buried in bags and suitcases and totes we didn't have time to sort — we'd wanted to leave no later than September 28th and that was that. Maggie's van life friend Maddie was coming with us in her own van, which made sense on a road as remote as the Alaska Highway. I watched Anchorage disappear in the side mirror as we drove out in the dark. Six months in that city. I came for a pilot's license and left with everything else.

The Alaska Highway through the Yukon is one of the most remote drives in the world. Long, flat, huge sky, almost no one else. Crossed at Beaver Creek. Maddie was about 45 minutes ahead of us most of the time.

Route 99, Sea to Sky Highway, BC
October 2–3, 2025
Woke up in Prince George on Oct 2nd and turned onto Route 99 for a Whistler detour. Brakes started failing on a steep descent — smoking at the bottom, ignored the signs. Hours later I was asleep in the back when Maggie woke me up screaming. Brakes completely gone, 25mph into a hairpin. She steered us into a gap in the trees — an unmarked runaway lane — and we stopped on the grass. Maddie drove back 45 minutes the next morning to jump our dead battery. Started on the last possible attempt before the tow truck loaded us.

Vancouver & North Vancouver, BC
~October 4–9, 2025
Parked at Stanley Park for the long weekend. Lime scooters, dispensaries, transit. Monday, Supertech Auto quoted $9,000 CAD to fix the brakes — a complete scam. Another shop called and said it was egregious. Dan's shop found the real problem: a blown brake line Supertech missed entirely. Half the pads, half the calipers, no rotors. Final bill $4,500 CAD ($3,300 USD).

the person behind this

Hey.
I'm Beckett.

I grew up in Raleigh, NC. In March 2025 I flew to Anchorage to get my pilot's license. I got a lot of other things instead — a summer in Alaska, a job launching floatplanes at Lake Hood, a girlfriend who lives in an ambulance, and a trip that took us from the Yukon to the Grand Canyon and back. This site is my attempt to not forget any of it.

what I actually believe
🌲
The earth matters
Old growth forests in BC, bears fishing at Katmai, the Grand Canyon at dawn — none of this is guaranteed to last. Every mile of this trip made me care more about protecting it.
🍄
Slow down
The best moments were the unplanned ones. Staying two extra days somewhere because it held us. Sleeping to waves on the Oregon Coast. Not everything needs a schedule.
🌿
Less is more
Everything I own fit in a Crosstrek. Turns out that's enough. The stuff you thought you needed mostly just gets in the way when you're trying to get somewhere.
✊
People over profit
Dan the mechanic in North Vancouver, Maddie turning around 45 minutes without being asked — these are the people that make things work. Not the guys quoting $9,000 for brakes.
